Overview

Objectives

  • Identify the five parts of an effective apology.
  • Differentiate between a good apology and a bad one.
  • Recognize some common pitfalls that weaken your apologies.

Step 1 — Take Ownership

What it means

Name exactly what you did. Say it plainly. No excuses. No shifting blame.

Why it matters

Clear ownership signals accountability, lowers defensiveness, and opens the door to repair.

Step 2 — Acknowledge the Impact

What it means

State the effect of your action. Be concrete and specific.

Why it matters

Validates the other person's experience. Shows you understand the consequences of your actions.

Examples that don’t name the impact

Consider why each is ineffective. Open to check your thinking.

  • “I had a legitimate reason for doing so.”

    Why ineffective: Ignores all consequences. Suggests the impact doesn't matter; only the excuse matters.

  • “I'll try to do better next time.”

    Why ineffective: Promises action too quickly. If you don't acknowledge the harm you caused, how can the other person trust you to honor your commitment?

  • “It wasn’t a big deal.”

    Why ineffective: Minimizes the impact and invalidates their experience.

Weak → Strong

Note: Do you really understand the impact of your actions? If not, just ask! The other person is probably dying to tell you.

Step 3 — Show Remorse

What it means

Express sincere regret for the harm you caused.

Why it matters

Signals you take the harm seriously.

Examples that don’t show remorse

Consider why each is ineffective. Open to check your thinking.

  • "My bad."

    Why ineffective: Too casual. Minimizes the harm done by moving past it too quickly.

  • "It's not like I did it on purpose."

    Why ineffective: Too defensive; borderline accusatory. This suggests the other person is wrong for being hurt.

  • “It was just a joke.”

    Why ineffective: Ignores the harm that was done. Focuses only on your intent.

  • “I regret that you felt offended by what I said.”

    Why ineffective: Shows no remorse for your actions. Instead, shifts blame to their emotions. Implies their feelings are problematic.

  • “I already apologized.”

    Why ineffective: Seeks one-sided closure. No expression of remorse. No genuine attempt at repair.

Weak → Strong

Note: Don't fake it. Lies won't build trust. Take time to reflect on the impact of your actions. What could you have done differently?

Examples that don’t offer a repair

  • “I’ll do better next time.”

    Why ineffective: Future intent only. No fix for the current harm.

  • “Let me explain why I jumped in.”

    Why ineffective: Offers only an excuse.

  • “I’ll try to make it up to you.”

    Why ineffective: Too vague. Without a specific "what" and "when," there's no actual attempt to mend the harm.

Weak → Strong (rewrite)

Note: Your repair attempt should match the impact. If you embarrassed someone publicly, you should apologize to them publicly.

Step 5 — Make a Future Commitment

What it means

Clearly state what you will do differently in the future. Be realistic.

Why it matters

Turns your apology into a plan. Attempts to follow-through on this plan will rebuild trust.

Examples that don’t make a real commitment

Consider why each is ineffective. Open to check your thinking.

  • “It won’t happen again.”

    Why ineffective: A promise without a plan. Sets you up to fail.

  • “I’ll do better.”

    Why ineffective: Too vague. Without defining what "better" means, it doesn't offer any specific behavioral change.

  • “I’ll be more mindful.”

    Why ineffective: Intention only. Lacks a concrete step or trigger.

  • “Trust me.”

    Why ineffective: Demands trust instead of working to rebuild it.

Weak → Strong

Note: Good commitments are specific and observable. It should be clear to everyone whether you are honoring your commitment.
